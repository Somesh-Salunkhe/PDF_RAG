
The proliferation of wearable devices has generated vast volumes of
egocentric video and sensor data for human activity recognition (HAR),
yet dataset annotation remains a critical bottleneck due to its labourintensive nature. 
This thesis investigates the use of open-source visionlanguage models (VLMs) for automated 
weak annotation of multimodal HAR datasets, extending the clustering-based framework of
Bock et al. [4], which reduces manual labeling to the inspection of
a small set of cluster centroids.
Two novel weak-annotation frameworks are developed and evaluated
across three egocentric HAR datasets — WEAR, ActionSense, and
Wetlab. The first employs X-CLIP to assign weak activity labels via
image-caption similarity matching between cluster-level visual embeddings 
and textual activity descriptions. The second leverages BLIP2’s instruction-prompted 
caption generation, with resulting captions
analyzed through n-gram, TF-IDF, and sequential pattern techniques
to derive weak activity labels. This investigation is preceded by a systematic visualization 
and performance evaluation of CLIP and DINOv2 embeddings from the original framework, 
providing a diagnostic baseline analysis of the underlying embedding space.
Results consistently show that image-pretrained vision foundation models produce embeddings 
dominated by background scene context rather
than activity-specific body motion, resulting in zero-shot annotation
accuracies below practically useful thresholds across all datasets and
models. This background bias is identified as the primary cause of poor annotation 
performance and challenges the assumption that CLIPlike representations are inherently 
suitable for egocentric activity recognition. These findings indicate that reliable automated 
weak annotation of HAR datasets will likely require video-native foundation models, motion-aware
 architectures, or targeted fine-tuning on egocentric data.
