 This thesis extends the work of Bock et al. in [4] by investigating the use of VLMs
to further automate the video annotation step, with the long-term goal of minimizing or removing the need for manual review 
of cluster centroids. Methodologically, the frameworks remain similar to Bock et al.[4] up to the point of clustering: 
egocentric videos from HAR datasets are preprocessed, embedded using a vision (vision-language) backbone, and clustered in
 embedding space using GMM algorithm. The key difference is that, instead of relying on a human annotator to watch
each cluster centroid clip, the language head of a VLM is used to generate textual captions for the representative clips. 
These captions are then intended to be processed via text analysis and feature engineering to map each cluster to one of the
known activity classes in the dataset, thereby turning the semi-automated scheme into a (theoretically) fully automated 
weak annotation framework. The first contribution of this thesis is a systematic visualization and quality
analysis of the clusters produced by the authors’ original weak annotation framework. Using dimensionality 
reduction and cluster inspection, the thesis examines how well the generated visual embeddings separate the 
workout activities and how the cluster structure changes under different backbones. This analysis provides, for
the first time, an in-depth diagnostic view of the embedding space underlying the
original weak-annotation framework, going beyond aggregate labeling accuracy
metrics that were reported in prior work.
The second contribution concerns the empirical evaluation of caption-based
annotation with an image-pretrained VLM. The initial hypothesis was that modern VLMs would generate sufficiently 
informative captions for egocentric workout
clips, such that text analysis (eg., keyword matching or similarity to class descriptions) could reliably map 
cluster centroids to activity labels. However, experiments
showed that off-the-shelf VLMs struggled to produce high-quality, activity-specific
captions for first-person workout videos: generated descriptions often focused on
static background elements, scenery, or camera motion rather than the actual exercise being performed. 
Similar observations about the limitations of current models
on egocentric data have been reported in recent work on activities of daily life(ADL) annotations and
 egocentric vision, where models tend to capture coarse
scene context but miss fine-grained actions without task-specific adaptation.
The third and arguably most important contribution is the identification and
detailed characterization of a critical failure in using image-pretrained VLMs and
vision foundation models for egocentric workout video annotation. Through cluster visualization, 
inspection of actual video clips and qualitative analysis of captions, the thesis demonstrates that the 
embeddings extracted by Bock et al. in [4]
and those produced in this work are heavily biased towards background appearance and scene context and 
only weakly capture the actual body movements or
exercise type. This finding challenges the implicit assumption in previous work
that CLIP-like vision embeddings are inherently suitable for encoding activities in
egocentric videos, and aligns with broader evidence that image-pretrained VLMs
may be ill-suited for fine-grained temporal action understanding without substantial adaptation or 
video-specific pretraining. This thesis corroborates this conclusion by the application of the same 
framework to three datasets namely WEAR,
ActionSense and Wetlab, suggesting that the problem is not specific to any one
dataset but generalized to all three egocentric datasets.
In summary, the major contributions of the thesis are: (i) a thorough visualization and analysis of 
the cluster structure underlying the existing weak-annotation
framework; (ii) an empirical evaluation showing that straightforward image-pretrained
VLM-based captioning is not yet sufficient to fully automate egocentric video annotation for HAR datasets; 
and (iii) evidence that vision embeddings from imagepretrained (and not task fine-tuned) VLMs and vision foundation 
models are strongly
biased towards background content and therefore poorly aligned with the activity labels needed for wearable HAR. 
These insights provide practical guidance
for future work on weak annotation of multimodal HAR datasets, indicating that
progress will likely require either video-specialized foundation models, explicit
motion-aware architectures, or targeted fine-tuning on egocentric activity data rather
than relying solely on generic image-pretrained VLMs.
This thesis is structured into six chapters. Chapter 2 establishes the theoretical background underpinning the 
research. Chapter 3 focuses on the visualization
and performance evaluation of the embeddings computed by the original authors,
serving as a baseline for this study. Starting from Chapter 4, the thesis transitions to presenting the original 
contributions of this research. Chapter 4 details the
two novel weak-annotation frameworks developed in this thesis for the X-CLIP
and BLIP-2 vision-language models. Chapter 5 discusses the experimental results
and provides a comprehensive performance evaluation of these newly developed
frameworks. Finally, Chapter 6 concludes by summarising the core findings, offering a final assessment of 
vision-language models for automated weak annotation
in HAR, and outlining directions for future research.
